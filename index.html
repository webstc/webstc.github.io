FAQ

When should my son/daughter begin preparing for his/her subject test?

I recommend beginning 2-3 months before the test date. For a May test date, tutoring usually begins in late February. For June, in late March.

What's a good plan for a junior who is finishing pre-calculus and needs to take the Math 2 Sat Subject test?

Most students would do well to sign up for the test in May and June. The student should start preparing in late February or March in order to take the test in May. If the student meets or exceeds her desired score on the May test, then she's done. If not, she can take the test again in June.

How hard is the Physics SAT Subject Test?

Pretty hard for most students. Students should try a practice test and see how they do.

How much do students usually improve on the SAT Subject Tests?

Much depends upon the student's study habits and motivation. Hard-working students tend to see an average of a 100 point increase over a period of two months of study. The point increase is rarely lower than that. Occasionally a very hard-working student improves much more than 100 points. Students who begin already at a high level (ie, above a 650) improve on average 10% (ie, from 700 to 750-770). Incremental improvements become increasingly difficult in the 700+ range.

My son or daughter isn't quite ready to take the test, but wants to sit it just to see what it's like. Do you think that's a good idea?

It's not a bad idea, but in my experience preparing for standardized exams has an element of sport. I always want my students to be ready to take the exam. I want them to be at the top of their game, so that win, lose, or draw, they feel they gave it their best shot.

What is your method for preparing students for standardized tests?

Students who ace the SATs and ACTs have a lot in common. They are typically familiar with the exam format and the types of questions being asked. They have mastered the subject matter underlying those questions and practice intelligent and creative multiple-choice test-taking strategies. They know roughly how many questions they need to answer correctly to get the score they are shooting for. This is the type of student that many who seek tutoring hope to become.

Although it is not possible for everyone to ace their exam, everyone CAN work to reach the top of their potential and improve their score. Here's the process which I think is an efficient way to achieving this.

First, the student takes a practice exam in realistic conditions: a timed tests without distractions in an unfamiliar location (NOT in the student's room). Second, the student reviews the exam systematically in order to become familiar with his or her weaknesses. This sounds easy, but is perhaps the most difficult and humbling parts of the whole process. Nobody likes to look at their mistakes and confusion head on, but this is exactly what a student must do. Third, the student addresses these underlying weaknesses, including test-taking anxieties. Then, the student returns to step one and takes a new simulated practice test. Repeating this cycle several times will build confidence and help the student achieve at the top of her potential on testing day.
